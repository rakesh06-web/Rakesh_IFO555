Digital Public Library of America (DPLA)

System Introduction

Digital Public Library of America (DPLA) is an open-access platform that provides free access to millions of digital resources from libraries, archives, and museums across the United States. It was founded in 2013 and developed by a collaboration of numerous libraries, archives, and museums with the goal of democratizing access to digital knowledge. DPLA exists to create a digital library that allows free and open access to millions of resources. It was started by Harvard University's Berkman Center for Internet & Society in 2010, with financial support from the Alfred P. Sloan Foundation, and has subsequently received funding from several foundations and government agencies, including the US National Endowment for the Humanities and the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation.

Context

DPLA aggregates metadata and digital objects from a vast array of partner institutions across the U.S. The platform supports a range of media types, including books, photographs, government records, and audio/video content. It provides a powerful search tool that allows users to search across a variety of media and types of content. The platform uses open-access policies to ensure resources remain free to the public.

Users

  • Researchers
  • Students
  • Librarians and Educators
  • General Public
  • Developers (via API access)

Content

DPLA offers a vast range of digital materials, including:

  • Books and Manuscripts
  • Photographs and Historical Documents
  • Audio and Video Recordings
  • Government Records and Archival Data

As of recent reports, DPLA hosts over 40 million digital items from thousands of partner organizations.

Unique Features & Discussion

  • Comprehensive search and filtering tools
  • Educational resources and curated exhibitions
  • Integration with partner libraries for seamless access
  • Public API for developers to build applications
